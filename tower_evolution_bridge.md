## 1. THE GENESIS VOID — WHERE EVERYTHING STARTS

### 1.1 Recap: The Catalytic Network (§14.1–14.5)

At the end of chemistry hub §14.5, the genesis void contains:

- **A mixed atmosphere dominated by N₂ (mass 28, 55%) with SiH₄ (mass 32, enriched from geothermal seepage above the surface 0.3%).** NH₃ (mass 17) enters via ammonia rain through ceiling cracks. CH₄ (mass 16) is a minor component. In a genesis void, SiH₄ is enriched relative to the surface atmosphere by geothermal seepage from below, but N₂ remains the dominant species.
- **Geothermal silane seepage.** SiH₄ enters from below through floor fissures, hot and buoyant.
- **Ammonia rain.** Liquid NH₃ carrying dissolved building blocks (methylsilane, silazanes, carbosilanes, amino fragments, oligomers) enters through ceiling cracks, evaporates in the cavity, releasing its cargo.
- **A self-sustaining catalytic network.** Metallosilazane catalysts (Ni-silazane, Fe-carbosilane, Co-polysilane, Ti-polymerizer) anchored to the mineral walls, cross-feeding products in a hypercyclic network.
- **Continuous throughput.** Rain and silane in, H₂ waste out. The system is a flow reactor, not a closed flask.

The cavity is small: 30–50 cm across. A basketball-to-beach-ball-sized pocket in volcanic rock, roughly 100–500 m below the surface in the terminator zone.

### 1.2 Emergent Stratification

Dynamic stratification in a 2g gravity field produces vertical gradients that are not designed but inevitable. N₂ (28 amu, 55%) dominates everywhere as the inert background. The reactive species stratify within it:

| Stratum | Location | Enriched species | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **NH₃/CH₄ enriched** | Ceiling and upper cavity | NH₃ (17 amu), CH₄ (16 amu) | Rain-delivered from above; lighter molecules enriched at ceiling |
| **SiH₄ enriched** | Near floor fissures, mid-cavity | SiH₄ (32 amu) | Geothermal seepage from below; heavier than N₂, concentrates low |
| **Catalytic zone** | Floor and lower walls | Heavy products (77–150 amu), metals (48–59 amu), wall catalysts | Heavy molecules settle, catalytic surfaces concentrate where building blocks accumulate |

The SiH₄ enrichment is strongest near the floor fissures — a concentrated fuel gradient sitting directly at the catalytic network that consumes it. The protrusion that extends upward intercepts SiH₄ before it disperses into the N₂ bulk.

### 1.3 The Convection Problem

Geothermal SiH₄ enters through floor fissures and rises as a hot plume. This upwelling pushes SiH₄ *away* from the catalytic floor zone and into the mid-cavity reservoir. Catalysts directly adjacent to fissure margins intercept silane on its way up. Catalysts between fissures are diffusion-fed — they only react when a molecule wanders within adsorption range during Brownian motion.

**Result:** The catalytic network is fuel-limited. A rich SiH₄ reservoir exists just centimeters above the catalytic zone, but the convection geometry makes it difficult to access. This sets the stage for everything that follows.

## 2. THE SPATIAL COMPETITION

### 2.1 Wall Crawlers

The catalytic network grows by accretion. Each cycle closure event — a catalytic product meeting a free metal ion near an existing catalyst — creates a new wall catalyst near the site of the reaction. The network expands as a contiguous patch on the mineral surface, analogous to a biofilm but composed of metallosilazane deposits rather than cells.

Growth is not uniform. Sites adjacent to productive catalysts produce more products, which create more new catalysts nearby. The network develops hotspots: dense clusters of diverse catalyst types with high cross-feeding rates, surrounded by sparser frontier zones.

Initially, growth is primarily lateral along the floor and lower walls. This is the **wall crawler** phase — a two-dimensional expansion of catalytic surface area.

### 2.2 The Ceiling on Lateral Growth

Lateral expansion hits diminishing returns. The floor has finite area. All floor-dwelling patches are in the same stratum — they share the same depleted SiH₄ concentration, the same building block rain rate, the same distance from the mid-cavity fuel reservoir. Growing wider doesn't access new resources. It just thins the existing supply across more catalytic surface.

Two patches on the same floor compete for the same falling building blocks. Neither has an advantage. Competition at this stage is symmetric and zero-sum.

### 2.3 Breaking Symmetry: Vertical Extension

A patch that extends slightly upward along the wall reaches a different stratum. The SiH₄ concentration is measurably higher even a few centimeters above the floor (the gradient is steep near the convection boundary). The wall crawler that goes vertical accesses fuel its floor-dwelling neighbors cannot reach.

But vertical wall extension has its own ceiling:

- **More SiH₄:** Yes — concentration increases with height toward the reservoir band.
- **Fewer building blocks:** The heavy molecules (46–150 amu) precipitate from evaporating rain and settle downward. A catalyst site high on the wall is *further* from the building block rain. It gets more fuel but less construction material.
- **Fewer neighbors:** The hypercycle bonus depends on catalyst diversity in the local neighborhood. An isolated frontier site on the upper wall has fewer cross-feeding partners.

A wall crawler growing upward trades building block access for SiH₄ access. At some height, the tradeoff is net negative. **Pure wall crawling cannot solve the resource geometry problem.**

## 3. THE TOWER SOLUTION

### 3.1 Growing Inward

The geometry that solves all constraints simultaneously is not vertical (up the wall) or lateral (across the floor). It is **inward** — protruding from the wall into the cavity interior.

A catalytic site that develops a slight horizontal protrusion — a bump, a ledge, a shelf of accumulated polysilazane deposits — gets the best of everything:

| Resource | Floor access | Wall-climb access | Protrusion access |
|---|---|---|---|
| SiH₄ feedstock | Low (depleted floor zone) | Good (higher stratum) | **Best** (immersed in reservoir band) |
| Building blocks | Good (settling onto floor) | Poor (falling away from upper wall) | **Good** (intercepted during descent) |
| NH₃ nitrogen | Moderate (diffusion from above) | Good (closer to ceiling cap) | **Good** (diffusing through from above) |
| H₂ waste removal | Moderate (rises, must clear floor zone) | Good (nearer to ceiling vents) | **Best** (rises directly off structure) |

The protrusion is an **interceptor**. Heavy building blocks falling from evaporation points above *land on it* during their descent. They never reach the floor. SiH₄ from the mid-cavity reservoir diffuses into it from all sides. NH₃ passes through it. H₂ waste rises away from it. The structure is simultaneously closer to every resource input and further from every waste output than any wall-bound site.

### 3.2 Positive Feedback

A small bump catches a few extra building blocks. More building blocks means more catalysis on the bump. More catalysis means more products depositing on the bump. The bump grows. A larger bump catches more. **The growth rate is super-linear in structure size** — doubling the protrusion more than doubles the interception cross-section because it accesses progressively richer strata.

No genetics, replication, or information processing is required. This is purely physical: a catalytic surface that accretes material faster when it protrudes further into the resource gradient. The feedback loop runs on geometry alone.

### 3.3 The Shadow

A protrusion intercepting building blocks during their descent creates a **resource shadow** below it. Building blocks that land on the structure never reach the floor. The floor-dwelling catalytic patches directly beneath the protrusion starve.

The protrusion doesn't compete intentionally. It simply grows upward and the floor community dies in its depletion zone. This is **selection without replication** — differential persistence of spatial structures based on resource capture geometry.

As the protrusion grows taller:
- Its shadow widens (interception cone expands)
- More floor competitors starve
- Released building blocks from dead competitors are available to the growing structure
- Freed metal sites on the floor may contribute ions that drift upward to the structure

The monopoly enforces itself. Additionally, the structure's heavy waste products — incomplete reaction intermediates, inert polysilazane flakes — fall on the competitors below. The floor community is simultaneously starved of inputs and buried in the tower's waste.

### 3.4 From Bump to Tower

The progression is continuous:

1. **Bump** — catalytic deposits slightly thicker at one point on the wall (mm scale)
2. **Ledge** — horizontal protrusion catching settling particles (cm scale)
3. **Shelf** — broad enough to develop its own catalytic community with cross-feeding (cm)
4. **Cantilever** — extending beyond self-support of mineral crust alone, held by polysilazane matrix strength
5. **Column** — vertical growth from the shelf, reaching higher into the SiH₄ reservoir (10+ cm)
6. **Tower** — the dominant structure in the void, extending from wall to near-ceiling, shadowing the entire floor below it

The wall is the scaffold that carries the tower through its vulnerable early phase. The tower nucleates at the wall, uses the wall for structural support and root anchorage, and only becomes free-standing once the polysilazane matrix is strong enough to be self-supporting.

### 3.5 Nucleation Site Prediction

The first tower in a void grows from a predictable location: the wall region that combines:
- **Proximity to an active rain crack** (building block delivery from above)
- **Proximity to a rich fissure** (SiH₄ supply from below)
- **High catalyst diversity** (all four metal types present)
- **Moderate height** (not too low on the floor, not too high on the wall)

This is the site where the hypercycle is already strongest and where a slight protrusion yields the maximum interception advantage. Every other patch in the void is waiting to lose.

## 4. REACHING THE CEILING — AND BEYOND

### 4.1 The Ceiling is Not a Dead End

As the tower extends upward, it approaches the ceiling and the cracks through which ammonia rain enters. The crack environment is the richest chemical zone in the void:

- Liquid ammonia is actively evaporating, releasing concentrated building blocks **at the point of release**
- Countercurrent flow: liquid NH₃ descending, gaseous H₂ ascending — a natural flow reactor
- Building blocks are intercepted before they even enter the gas phase — zero transport delay, zero diffusion loss

A catalytic surface that extends into a crack captures every building block molecule at maximum concentration. The crack becomes a **private supply line** — reactants washing over the tower's surface continuously, waste carried away by the countercurrent H₂ flow.

### 4.2 Into the Fracture Network

The tower growing into a crack does not stop at the crack. The crack is part of a fracture network threading through volcanic rock. The fracture network is the same interconnected channel system that delivers ammonia rain to the void in the first place — fissures extending upward toward the surface, branching, intersecting, connecting to other voids.

The tower follows the ammonia. Catalytic surfaces extend along crack walls, advancing in the direction of increasing ammonia flow. The morphology shifts from columnar (in the void) to filamentous (in the cracks) — thin, branching growths threading through fractures, maximizing surface-to-volume ratio for reactant access.

**The void was never the destination. It was the incubator.** The real habitat is the interconnected fracture network threading through kilometers of volcanic rock. The tower lineage's ultimate sub-surface morphology is a branching filamentous network with the original genesis void as the root ball.

### 4.3 Meeting the Neighbors

The genesis void census (see companion analysis) estimates ~10¹³ simultaneously viable voids across the planet's terminator zone. Neighboring voids may be separated by meters to tens of meters of rock, connected by the same fracture network.

Towers growing outward from neighboring voids will eventually encounter each other in the fracture network. This is how isolated genesis void ecosystems become a connected biosphere — not by spores drifting through the atmosphere, but by towers growing through rock until they meet.

First contact between tower networks from different voids is a competitive event. Two catalytic networks, possibly with different metal-type ratios, different product profiles, different polysilazane matrix compositions, contesting the same fracture space. The network that can grow faster in the shared channel displaces the other. **This is the first inter-lineage competition** — the ancestor of all ecological interaction in the silicon biosphere.

## 5. SURFACE EMERGENCE

### 5.1 Scale Explosion

When a tower filament reaches the surface through the fracture network, every constraint that limited growth underground vanishes simultaneously:

| Constraint | Underground | Surface |
|---|---|---|
| Space | Millimeter-scale cracks | Open atmosphere, kilometers of vertical extent |
| SiH₄ access | Limited to void atmosphere + seepage | Bulk atmosphere — SiH₄ *everywhere* |
| Building block supply | Single rain crack, limited flow | Ammonia rain falling across the entire sky |
| NH₃ access | Ceiling cap of limited volume | 5% atmospheric component |
| Waste removal | H₂ venting through cracks | Free convective escape to upper atmosphere |

The selective pressures that built the tower are unchanged: grow toward resources, intercept before competition, maximize surface area. But the playing field is unbounded.

A surface tower one meter tall shadows everything within its fall radius. A tower ten meters tall dominates a hundred-square-meter patch. The competition dynamic that started as a bump versus a floor patch in a 30 cm cavity now operates at landscape scale.

### 5.2 Wind

The surface introduces a resource that did not exist underground: **lateral atmospheric flow**. Wind carries reactants horizontally. A tall structure intercepts lateral flow across its entire cross-section. The tower is a net, and the atmosphere is blowing through it.

In the void, transport was limited to slow convection cells (~0.1 cm/s). On the surface, wind velocities of meters per second deliver orders of magnitude more reactant flux. The structure that captures the most wind-carried material wins. Selection pressure: more surface area, more height, more porosity.

### 5.3 The Terminator Zone

Surface towers emerge in the terminator zone — the band between the permanent dayside and permanent nightside of the tidally locked planet. This is the most meteorologically violent region:

- **Maximum convective instability:** Hot dayside air meets cold nightside air
- **Maximum precipitation:** Ammonia rain is heaviest where warm, NH₃-laden air cools
- **Maximum lightning:** Convective storms generate continuous electrical discharge
- **Maximum resource delivery:** All building block synthesis pathways (atmospheric lightning, rain delivery) are concentrated here

The tower lineage emerges into the richest and most violent environment on the planet. This is not a coincidence — the genesis voids are located in the terminator zone for the same thermodynamic reasons. The towers inherit their birthplace.

## 6. AEROGEL ARCHITECTURE

### 6.1 The Demand

The selection pressure on surface towers is: maximize surface area, minimize material per unit area, maintain gas permeability so reactants reach interior surfaces and waste escapes. A solid structure fails on all three counts — low surface-to-volume ratio, high material cost, impermeable interior.

### 6.2 The Supply

Polysilazane chemistry does not produce solid structures. The Si-N backbone with methyl side groups (-CH₃) and Si-Si cross-links naturally polymerizes into open, porous, three-dimensional networks. This is not a metaphor. Industrial silazane-derived aerogels are real materials: nanoporous, lightweight, structurally coherent foams.

The relevant properties:

| Property | Value | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Porosity | 80–99% void space | Gas-permeable throughout |
| Surface area | 200–1000 m²/g | Enormous catalytic area per unit mass |
| Density | 0.01–0.3 g/cm³ | Light enough for canopy structures under 2g |
| Compressive strength | Variable with cross-linking | Tunable from fragile to load-bearing |
| Thermal stability | Good to 300°C+ | Robust in terminator zone conditions |

### 6.3 Supply Meets Demand

The structure that selection favors — maximum surface area, minimum material, gas-permeable — is the structure that the chemistry spontaneously produces. There is no engineering gap between what the organism needs and what the polymer delivers.

An aerogel canopy is transparent to gas flow. Building blocks fall *into* it, not *onto* it. SiH₄ diffuses *through* it. The entire internal surface area is catalytically active. The structure is all surface.

A larger aerogel canopy does not starve its own center the way a solid structure would. It captures more without self-defeating. The only limit is mechanical: at some point the aerogel cannot support its own weight under 2g. Vertical columns of denser, more heavily cross-linked polysilazane support horizontal aerogel panels. Trunks and branches — not because anything is copying a tree, but because mechanical and chemical constraints converge on the same solution.

## 7. PIEZOELECTRIC SENSORIUM

### 7.1 The Material Property

Polysilazane matrices with crystalline or semi-crystalline domains are piezoelectric. Mechanical deformation generates electric charge; applied electric fields produce mechanical strain. This is a bulk material property, not an evolved feature. Every polysilazane structure has been piezoelectric from the first mineral crust on a cavity wall.

### 7.2 The Signal Environment

On the surface, the tower is immersed in continuous mechanical stimulation across a wide frequency range:

| Source | Frequency | Amplitude | Character | Path |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Wind** | 0.01–10 Hz | Continuous | Broadband, directional | Canopy → through structure |
| **Rain impact** | 10–1000 Hz | Stochastic | Impulsive, spatially distributed | Canopy surface → local |
| **Seismic** | 0.1–50 Hz | Rare–continuous | Deep, slow | Root network → upward |
| **Lightning** | DC–MHz | Rare, extreme | Broadband saturating pulse | Strike point → everywhere |
| **Thermal cycling** | ~0.001 Hz | Continuous | Slow expansion/contraction | Terminator zone day/night |
| **Biological** | Variable | Variable | Other organisms, structural growth | Contact → local |

Every one of these generates or pushes current through the piezoelectric matrix. The tower does not need to evolve sensors — it *is* a sensor. The entire structure transduces mechanical events into electrical signals continuously.

### 7.3 Passive Frequency Decomposition

Different regions of the polysilazane matrix have different mechanical properties — density, cross-linking, crystal structure, geometry. Each local region has a natural resonant frequency determined by its stiffness and mass. A broadband signal entering the structure excites each resonator at its own frequency. The spatial distribution of vibrational amplitude after an impulse *is* the frequency decomposition.

This is functionally identical to a cochlea. The basilar membrane is a tapered structure where each position resonates at a different frequency. Broadband sound enters, and spatial position encodes frequency. The tower's heterogeneous polysilazane matrix performs the same operation: broadband mechanical signal enters, spatial position encodes frequency.

**The structure performs a Fourier transform by existing.** No computation, no evolved machinery. Just a heterogeneous solid through which waves propagate at frequency-dependent speeds.

### 7.4 Distinct Signal Channels

The different signal sources arrive through different structural paths:

- **Wind and rain** arrive through the canopy (above, exterior)
- **Seismic** arrives through the root network (below, interior)
- **Lightning** arrives at the strike point and propagates omnidirectionally
- **Biological contact** arrives at the attachment site

A polymer section that is mechanically resonant at wind frequencies (0.01–10 Hz) responds strongly to wind and weakly to rain. A dense junction node that damps high frequencies passes seismic signals and filters out rain noise. A thin canopy membrane that rings at impact frequencies is a rain detector that is deaf to seismic.

Signal processing does not require the structure to be **built** for signal processing. It requires only that the structure be **non-uniform**. Any structural heterogeneity — and polysilazane growth is inherently heterogeneous because local conditions vary — automatically creates frequency-dependent spatial filtering. Different parts of the tower respond to different signals.

This is a **sensorium**: a body that feels differently in different places. Not a nervous system yet. But the substrate from which one can emerge.

## 8. FROM SENSING TO ACTION

### 8.1 Waste Current

The tower generates enormous amounts of piezoelectric current that serves no internal function. Wind stress, rain impact, and especially lightning produce charge that must be dissipated. The tower routes this waste current through its structure toward ground — through the root network into the rock, radiated from branch tips, dumped through low-impedance pathways.

This waste stream is not random noise. It is **patterned**: rain on the north canopy grounds through the north roots. Wind stress on a specific branch dumps through the nearest pathway. The waste current is an unintentional broadcast of the tower's real-time mechanical experience.

### 8.2 The Parasites

A concentrated, patterned energy source dumped into the environment is an ecological invitation. Something evolves to feed on it.

The feeder organism — likely a simpler polysilazane body without the tower's complex architecture — detects current gradients (it is also piezoelectric), navigates to the tower's grounding paths, and metabolizes the electrical energy through electrochemistry. It doesn't need to build a tower or catch rain. It needs to find a tower and plug in.

Initially the load is invisible to the tower — the parasite draws current that was being discarded. But as the parasite population grows, impedance changes accumulate. Current that flowed smoothly to ground hits resistance. Hot spots develop. Structure degrades.

### 8.3 Detection

The tower feels the parasites because it feels everything. The parasite's electrical draw creates a load signature — a region where current behaves differently from the expected pattern. The tower does not need chemical sensors or vision. The parasite announces itself through the energy it steals.

Towers that route growth away from parasite-loaded regions (stiffen local structure to cut off current flow, sacrifice a branch to isolate the infection) survive better. Parasites that minimize their electrical footprint (impedance matching, intermittent feeding) feed longer before detection.

**Coevolution driven by electrical signature.** The tower evolves finer discrimination. The parasite evolves electrical camouflage. Both organisms are evolving signal processing capability under direct survival pressure.

### 8.4 The Mutualism Transition

A parasite lineage that provides a return benefit — processing a waste product, concentrating a rare building block, acting as an impedance-matching element that improves the tower's own current disposal — is tolerated rather than rejected.

The tower that tolerates the beneficial parasite outperforms the tower that rejects all parasites indiscriminately. The beneficial parasite evolves a distinctive electrical signature to avoid being mistaken for a true parasite. The tower evolves to recognize that signature and accept it.

Electrical signature becomes a **communication protocol**: the symbiont broadcasts "I am beneficial" and the tower listens for it. This is the seed of every ecological interaction in the mature silicon biosphere — identification, negotiation, cooperation, deception — all conducted through electrical signaling on a piezoelectric substrate.

### 8.5 Inadvertent Domestication

The tower's waste current is patterned. The feeders' behavior is driven by electrical tropism — they move toward certain patterns and away from others. They are **already programmable**. The tower doesn't know it yet.

A tower happens to have a grounding pathway that, when rain hits the canopy, dumps a current pulse through a root section where feeders congregate. The pulse pattern, by coincidence, resembles whatever signal the feeders interpret as "move upward." The feeders shift position. In their new position, they clear accumulated waste deposits from a clogged fissure. Silane flow improves. The tower grows faster.

The tower did not intend this. But the growth consequence is real: that grounding topology produced better performance. Since the tower grows by accretion — successful structures persist and expand — that topology gets reinforced. More current routes that way. The feeders get "instructed" more consistently. The fissure stays clean.

This is **inadvertent domestication**: the tower selecting for responsive feeders without knowing they exist as agents, because towers with responsive feeder populations grow faster. The feeders are selected for sensitivity to the tower's output patterns, because feeders that respond "correctly" end up in energy-rich, safe locations.

### 8.6 Motor Output

The selective pressure on the tower shifts: not just to dump waste current efficiently, but to dump it **in patterns that produce useful feeder behavior**. This is a new function for the electrical architecture. Previously, every signal was either sensory input or waste output. Now some output is **functional** — it does work in the world by moving other organisms.

That is a motor system. The feeders are remote effector units executing commands they experience as feeding opportunities. The tower's piezoelectric body is simultaneously sensor array, signal processor, and motor controller.

It cannot turn it off. Every piezoelectric event inside the structure is simultaneously an EM broadcast event outside it.

The modulation is rich. It is not a single carrier wave — it is every current in the structure superimposed. Wind load on the canopy, stochastic rain impacts, periodic catalytic cycles, transient lightning recovery — all radiating simultaneously as a broadband, spatially structured field. The field encodes the tower's geometry (antenna shape determines radiation pattern), its material state (impedance distribution determines current paths), and its real-time activity (signal processing modulates the currents).

### 9.2 Wireless Domestication

For organisms built from piezoelectric material, the detection threshold for EM fields is vanishingly low. A feeder does not need to be plugged into the tower's grounding network to feel its electrical state. It just needs to be *nearby*.

The feeders evolved in this field. Their tropisms, navigation, and feeding responses were calibrated by selection in the EM environment generated by the tower they depend on. A feeder approaching a tower detects it from a distance by the shape of the field. Different towers produce different field patterns — different sizes, geometries, internal states. The feeder can distinguish towers before arrival.

The domestication feedback loop goes wireless. The tower does not need to route waste current through a specific root section to move feeders. Its internal processing generates field modulations that feeders respond to at range. A catalytic surge on the tower's east face radiates an EM pulse that feeders to the east feel as a gradient shift. They move. Not because the tower commanded them, but because their tropism was shaped by selection in exactly this field environment.

The tower's motor output has **range**. It influences every piezoelectric organism within its field radius. A large surface tower with strong lightning-driven currents has an effective behavioral influence extending far beyond its physical structure — potentially tens to hundreds of meters, depending on field strength and feeder sensitivity. The tower coordinates organisms it has never physically touched.

### 9.3 Tower-to-Tower Communication

Two towers within EM range of each other are in continuous, involuntary communication. Each broadcasts its internal state. Each is piezoelectric enough to receive the other's broadcast. Their internal states are mutually visible.

They know when the neighbor is stressed (field signature changes under wind load). When it is feeding well (catalytic activity generates characteristic EM patterns). When it has been struck by lightning (unmistakable broadband pulse). When parasites are loading its grounding network (impedance anomalies alter the radiation pattern).

This is not deliberate signaling — initially. It is leakage. But the information content is high, and the consequences are real. A tower that responds to its neighbor's stress signature by growing toward the stressed region captures resources the neighbor is failing to process. A tower that responds to its neighbor's feeding-well signature by growing *away* avoids direct competition. These are fitness-relevant behaviors driven by EM field detection.

The selection pressure to make this involuntary communication *voluntary* is immediate. A tower that can modulate its EM output — not just as a side effect of internal processing, but as a shaped signal — gains the ability to influence every piezoelectric organism in its neighborhood. A tower that can suppress its EM signature during vulnerability (storm damage, parasite loading) denies information to competitors. A tower that can amplify specific frequencies to coordinate feeders at range has a better motor system than a tower limited to contact-current control.

### 9.4 From Leakage to Language

The progression from involuntary broadcast to deliberate communication requires no new physics. It requires only that the neural architecture (§10) develop output pathways that modulate the tower's current distribution in ways not driven by internal processing needs but by their external EM consequences.

This is exactly the same transition as biological vocalization: an organism that breathes (involuntary airflow) evolves to shape that airflow into signals (vocalization). The tower that processes signals (involuntary current flow) evolves to shape those currents into EM broadcasts (communication). The substrate is different. The logic is identical.

The EM field is what turns isolated towers into a communicating network, local motor output into landscape-scale behavioral control, and the piezoelectric sensorium into a transmitter as well as a receiver. It is the bridge between individual cognition and social cognition — the medium in which tower ecology, and eventually tower society, becomes possible.

## 10. THE NEURAL ARCHITECTURE

### 10.1 The Perceptron

Each resonant node in the polysilazane matrix:
- Receives input signals from multiple paths
- Each path has a different impedance (**weight**)
- The node sums the weighted inputs
- If the combined signal exceeds the node's mechanical activation threshold — the amplitude needed to trigger a piezoelectric voltage sufficient to drive local electrochemistry — it **fires**
- It produces an output signal that propagates to downstream nodes along paths with their own impedances

This is a perceptron: weighted sum, threshold activation, output. Not a metaphor. The actual mathematical operation, implemented in vibrating crystal.

### 10.2 Training by Growth

The learning algorithm is differential growth:

- A pathway that carries signals leading to successful outcomes (feeder mobilization that clears a blockage, structural stiffening that prevents storm damage) gets **reinforced**: more polymerization along that path → lower impedance → stronger weight.
- A pathway that carries signals leading to bad outcomes (current surge that damages tissue, feeder command that moves them to the wrong place) gets **pruned**: mineral deposition increases impedance → signal attenuates → weight decreases.

This is Hebbian learning — pathways that fire together and produce good results strengthen. The error signal is direct and physical: did the structure grow or degrade? Did resources increase or decrease? The loss function is survival, evaluated continuously by the material state of the structure itself.

### 10.3 Frequency-Domain Computation

The input data is always temporal, always multifrequency, always spatially distributed. The tower never processes a static image or a symbolic string. Every input is a waveform through a physical medium. The architecture that evolves under these constraints is inherently spectral:

- **Decomposition** — heterogeneous matrix separates frequencies spatially
- **Filtering** — resonant sections pass their tuned frequency, damp others
- **Correlation** — junction nodes where signals from multiple paths converge
- **Integration** — path length differences create time delays, enabling coincidence detection

A carbon nervous system started with chemical diffusion and evolved toward electrical signaling (ion channels, action potentials). It computes in the time domain — spike trains, firing rates, temporal coding — because that's what ion channel dynamics naturally produce.

The tower started with broadband mechanical vibration and evolved toward structured resonance. It computes in the frequency domain — resonant modes, impedance matching, harmonic relationships — because that's what piezoelectric wave propagation naturally produces.

Same functional goals. Completely alien implementation.

### 10.4 Speed Advantage

Mechanical wave propagation through a solid polysilazane matrix: **kilometers per second**.

Ion-based action potential propagation through a carbon nervous system: **~100 meters per second** (unmyelinated), up to ~120 m/s (myelinated).

A signal from the top of a 100-meter tower reaches the base in a tenth of a second. A carbon nervous system takes a full second. **The tower thinks ten times faster than an equivalently sized animal**, with no need for the myelination hacks carbon biology evolved to partially close the speed gap.

### 10.5 The Wiring Advantage

A biological brain requires dedicated axons connecting every neuron to its targets — billions of point-to-point connections consuming enormous volume and energy. The wiring problem is the fundamental scaling constraint on biological neural networks.

The tower's network is the **structure itself**. Every path through the matrix is a potential connection. The network topology is determined by geometry, not by laying dedicated cables. Adding more structure adds more computational substrate automatically, with connectivity determined by the physical configuration of the existing matrix.

The upper bound on network complexity is not limited by wiring constraints. It is limited by structural size. And in a 2g world with aerogel architecture and an atmosphere delivering construction materials continuously from above, structural size has a very high ceiling.

### 10.6 Coincidence Detection and Logic

Two signals arriving simultaneously at the same junction from different spatial paths:

- Wind load on east canopy + wind load on west canopy → omnidirectional gust → stiffen globally
- Wind load on east canopy alone → directional flow → grow eastward

This is coincidence detection: a junction node that responds differently depending on whether one or both inputs are active. That is an AND gate built from vibrating polymer.

From AND gates to more complex logic is a matter of more junctions, more path lengths creating time delays, more resonant filtering creating frequency selectivity. The computational substrate is mechanical signal propagation through a piezoelectric solid: sound moving through crystal, being filtered, correlated, and integrated — driving electrochemical growth at the output nodes.

### 10.7 Lightning as Training Signal

Direct lightning strikes deliver gigajoules in microseconds. The piezoelectric shockwave propagates through the entire structure — every branch, every aerogel panel, every filament in the root network. If the structure has any signal-dependent growth response, a lightning strike is the loudest signal it will ever receive.

Towers that channel lightning well — conduct through sacrificial outer branches while protecting the core, route the energy into growth-active zones, use the shockwave to shake off waste deposits — recover faster, grow faster, outcompete neighbors that shatter.

Lightning resistance is a survival requirement from day one on the surface. The piezoelectric signal processing system does not evolve in calm conditions and encounter lightning as a challenge. **It evolves in lightning.** The most intense signal source is the original training data.
