**5.1 The Aerogel Lattice**

*\[NEW --- V2.2 --- supersedes earlier references to basket-weave or
branching lattice\]*

**✓ RESOLVED:** The tower lattice is a mineral aerogel --- a foam of
semiconductor deposited at nanoscale to microscale. Mostly void by
volume. Enormous internal surface area. Strong in compression because
load paths distribute through the entire foam matrix. Light enough that
kilometer-tall columns are feasible in 1.82g precisely because the
material is mostly air.

The aerogel structure resolves the competing demands of 1.82g tower
construction: surface area (for gas exchange, photovoltaic capture, and
particulate trapping), structural strength (compression loading of a
kilometer-tall column), and low density (to keep total mass feasible in
high gravity). No macroscopic branching geometry satisfies all three.
Branches in 1.82g cannot extend far from the central axis without
catastrophic bending loads. Brain-coral convolution provides surface
area but at excessive density. Aerogel provides all three: enormous
internal surface area, compressive strength through distributed load
paths, and low bulk density.

The symbiotes do not sit on the surface of a structure. They live
throughout the material, because the material is open at their scale.
Gas diffuses through the aerogel matrix the way air moves through a
sponge. Every internal surface is available for silane metabolism.
Photovoltaic capture happens throughout the volume, not just on the
exterior. The whole tower is a lung and a solar panel simultaneously, at
every point.

Amorphs inhabit macroscopic voids within the aerogel matrix ---
chambers, passages, hollows where the deposition pattern left larger
spaces or where organisms carved them. The structural material between
chambers is aerogel. The structure is hierarchical: nanoscale foam makes
the material, macroscale voids make the habitat. From outside, the
surface looks almost like stone --- rough, mineral, massive-seeming.
Pick up a piece and it is shockingly light. Break it open and you see
foam --- an impossibly fine mineral sponge, voids too small to resolve
with the naked eye.

**5.2 The Self-Recycling Windward Face**

*\[NEW --- V2.2 --- supersedes earlier sacrificial-crust model\]* **[SUPERSEDED v2.4+ — Wind does NOT shatter struts. **✓ RESOLVED:** The windward face of a tower in wind is not eroded in
the conventional sense. Wind shatters individual struts in the nanoscale
foam matrix, liberating dust-sized mineral fragments. These fragments
are caught by the wind --- which is blowing into the face --- and travel
microns to millimeters before reembedding in the porous matrix
immediately behind. The symbiotes there metabolize the reembedded
material. The windward face inhales its own ablated dust.

This is not a calving-and-rebuilding cycle. It is continuous churning at
the particle scale. Material liberated from the outermost struts is
carried the smallest possible distance and redeposited in the matrix
immediately behind. Recycling is continuous, at the dust scale, faster
than any compaction or rind-formation process. No armor layer builds up.
No rind seals the surface.

The windward hole geometry differs from the leeward. The leeward face
has open pores shaped by biological growth --- symbiotes depositing
mineral in their natural pattern. The windward face has smaller,
irregular pores shaped by the stochastic process of dust impact and
reembedding. Denser but not sealed. Different microstructure, same
function. Like bone remodeled under stress versus bone grown in quiet
conditions --- same material, different texture, both alive.

Net erosion is limited to the fraction of dust fine enough and light
enough to escape the surface entirely --- carried up and over the tower
and lost downwind. This fraction is tiny. Migration is driven by this
minimal loss on the windward face combined with net accretion on the
leeward face, where fresh atmospheric particulates are trapped in the
porous matrix from the airstream. Migration is glacial but nonzero.

The windward face is not sealed, starved, or armored. It is the tower's
primary feeding surface --- facing directly into the particulate stream,
with wind pressure actively pushing atmosphere and particles into the
matrix. The tower eats through its windward face. It breathes through
its windward face. It just also loses a tiny fraction of its own dust
from that face. The aerogel surface is a particle trap on all sides. The
entire tower is a mouth.

**10.3.4 Tower Nucleation: The Ping-Latency Trigger**

*\[NEW --- V2.2\]*

**✓ RESOLVED:** New towers nucleate from the surface root network when
signal latency between the advancing root edge and the nearest existing
tower crosses a critical threshold. Every meter of root between the
network edge and the nearest tower adds delay. The root mineral is
conductive but thin --- low bandwidth, measurable propagation time. At
sufficient distance, the round-trip signal from the network edge to the
nearest tower and back degrades network coherence at that point.

The symbiotes' response to this condition is not a decision. It is the
same thing they always do: deposit mineral. But at the latency
threshold, vertical accretion is rewarded by the network's own signal
environment. A growing heap of mineral becomes a better antenna, a
better relay, a better node. Vertical growth shortens the effective ping
for everything around it. The heap becomes a tower.

Two cases of tower formation are therefore distinguished only by their
root source:

**First-generation towers:** The deep-rock colony reaches the surface
through a fracture. Surface-facing organisms contact atmospheric silane
and begin vertical accretion. The deep-rock colony is the root system
from day one. No surface root network yet exists. This is the origin
event for any given region's tower forest.

**Network-generation towers:** The surface root network, extending from
established towers, reaches the latency threshold. Symbiotes at that
point begin accreting vertically. The existing network is the root
system from day one. The new tower is connected, fed, and supported from
the moment of nucleation.

The mechanism is identical in both cases --- symbiotes encountering
favorable conditions for vertical mineral deposition. The difference is
the substrate they grow from (fracture outlet vs. surface root) and the
support infrastructure available (none vs. mature logistics network).

**10.3.6 Tower Spacing: Wind-Modified Fibonacci Packing**

*\[NEW --- V2.2\]*

**✓ RESOLVED:** Tower spacing is self-regulating and follows a
wind-modified Fibonacci distribution. The nucleation rule --- new tower
appears where the ping is longest, i.e., where the root network is
farthest from any existing tower --- is mathematically equivalent to the
mechanism producing Fibonacci phyllotaxis in plants (new growth point
nucleates farthest from existing growth points, which create inhibition
zones around themselves).

Each tower creates a latency inhibition zone: the area within its
ping-latency threshold where no nucleation occurs because the network is
adequately served. The packing solution that maximizes coverage while
maintaining latency tolerance tends toward hexagonal spacing, which is
the geometric domain of Fibonacci distributions.

The katabatic wind breaks radial symmetry. Root extension is faster
day-ward (the tower's migration direction carries symbiotes and material
downwind). Lateral extension perpendicular to the wind is slower.
Inhibition zones are not circles but ellipses elongated in the wind
direction. The resulting tower distribution is a modified Fibonacci
packing: compressed crosswind, stretched downwind.

Viewed from altitude, the tower forest does not resemble a radially
symmetric sunflower head. It resembles one pulled like taffy in the wind
direction. But the underlying mathematical relationships between tower
positions --- the angle ratios, the packing densities, the self-similar
scaling --- carry Fibonacci signatures.

Tower spacing varies by region. Spacing is determined by signal
propagation characteristics of the local root mineral (conductivity,
cross-section) and the network's latency tolerance. Regions with
higher-quality root mineral (better conductivity) have wider tower
spacing. Regions with poor root mineral have denser tower forests. The
deposition zone, with its rich mineral feedstock, may have wider spacing
than the terminator corridor, where root mineral is continuously eroded
and redeposited.

**⚠ NOTE:** This Fibonacci distribution is not a design choice. It is an
unavoidable physical consequence of the nucleation mechanism. Any system
in which new nodes appear where existing node coverage is weakest, and
each node creates an inhibition zone, converges on this packing
geometry. The wind-modified variant is specific to this planet's
unidirectional katabatic flow.

**NEW ENTRIES: DEPOSITION ZONE ECOLOGY**

**10.3.1 Deposition Zone Shadowing**

*\[NEW --- V2.2\]*

As migrating towers arrive at the deposition zone and stop, newer
arrivals accumulate in front of older towers (relative to the
terminator). Over geological time, newer towers shadow older towers from
the incoming particulate stream. Front-row towers intercept airborne
material; back-row towers receive progressively less.

Front-row towers have a compounding advantage: more intercepted material
means faster growth, which means larger interception area, which means
more material. Positive feedback. The leading edge of the deposition
zone becomes a wall --- the densest, fastest-growing towers on the
planet.

Older towers deeper in the zone are alive, still metabolizing
atmospheric silane, still part of the network. But their bonus material
supply --- the pre-processed particulate rain from the terminator --- is
intercepted by the front row. They grow slower. Their material wealth is
largely fossil: accumulated early, when the corridor between terminator
and deposition zone was less crowded.

**⚠ NOTE:** Migrating towers in the terminator-to-deposition corridor
also skim particulates from the airstream. Every tower in transit
metabolizes and permanently locks some intercepted material into its
lattice. The cumulative skim means less material reaches the deposition
zone each era than the era before. The deposition zone's richest period
was its earliest, when the corridor was empty.

**10.3.2 The Ant Logistics Response to Shadowing**

*\[NEW --- V2.2\]*

The ant logistics system (see Section 10.3.3) resolves the shadowing
problem without any new adaptation. Shadowed deposition-zone towers sit

symbiote layer metabolizes it into structural mineral.

The network's management of these ants is logistical rather than
suppressive. In most tower ecologies, mobile middle-kingdom fauna are
pests --- the network's EM modulation discourages overgrazing of
photovoltaic surfaces. In the deposition zone, the same signal protocol
directs ant activity toward material transport. The network is running
the same system it has always run. The context --- abundant ground-level
mineral, shadowed airspace --- is what makes the behavior look like
farming.

the ground behind the tower. The trail connects to the next tower back,
which connects to the next, forming a surface-level network linking
every tower along the migration corridor. The deep-rock colony is the

sufficient for network participation. The tower does not need to
maintain deep-root contact to remain connected. A migrating tower is

**The Slime Trail as Physical Feature**

Every migrating tower leaves behind a continuous mineralized trail ---
conductive biomineral deposited at the tower's base, abandoned as the
tower moves day-ward. The trail is a physical record of every position
the tower has ever occupied. At a migration rate of roughly a millimeter
per year, a trail stretching a thousand kilometers represents a billion
years of tower history, written on the ground in conductive mineral.

The trails are permanent. In a reducing atmosphere with no free oxygen,
the conductive mineral does not corrode or oxidize. The trail deposited
a billion years ago is still there, still conductive, still physically
part of the network even though no living symbiote maintains it. The
mineral's conductivity is a material property, not a biological
function.

The ground between the terminator and the deposition zone is not bare
scoured peneplain. It is written on --- a palimpsest of mineralized
surface trails left by every tower that has ever migrated through.
Billions of years of trails, overlapping, crossing, branching where
towers split or merged lineages. The network's wiring diagram is
scratched into the surface of the planet.

**Trail Erosion and Persistence**

At the terminator, the katabatic wind erodes surface trails as it erodes
everything else. The newest trail section (immediately behind the tower)
is intact; older sections are progressively degraded. Trail persistence
at the terminator is limited --- centuries to millennia, not geological
time. The wind that drives migration also erases the record.

In the deposition zone, where erosion is zero, trails persist
indefinitely. The ground beneath and between the stopped towers is a
dense, ancient, three-dimensional archive of the entire migration
history of the tower lineage. Every trail that ever arrived is still
there, buried under newer deposits but physically intact. The deposition
zone's subsurface is a fossil record of planetary tower geography,
written in conductive mineral, potentially readable by monks with
trained somatic awareness.

**Trail Width and the Migration Corridor**

A tower's trail is not a thin line. The base of a tower is broad, and
mineral deposition spreads laterally. The trail width approximates the
tower's base diameter. Over geological time, overlapping trails from
successive towers migrating along similar paths merge into broad
conductive bands --- highways of mineral connecting the terminator to
the deposition zone. These bands carry more current than any single
trail and represent the network's highest-bandwidth surface connections
outside the deposition zone itself.

Migration corridors --- paths where towers preferentially nucleate and
travel, determined by underlying geology, fracture network outlets, and
wind patterns --- are marked on the surface by the densest trail
accumulations. The planet's transportation geography is visible from
altitude: dark mineralized bands radiating day-ward from the terminator,
converging on the deposition zone.

**10.4.2 Bandwidth and Maturation**

*\[NEW --- V2.2\]*

**✓ RESOLVED:** Network bandwidth at any tower scales with connection
density, which scales with time. A newly arrived tower in the deposition
zone, connected to the network only by its own migration trail, is
bandwidth-limited. Over time, it grows roots laterally into neighboring
towers' root systems. The local mesh thickens. Bandwidth increases. The
tower becomes a full computational participant.

A surface trail is a single conductive trace --- a straw. The deep-rock
colony is a three-dimensional fracture network threading through
kilometers of rock --- a trunk line. A tower connected only by surface
trail is on low bandwidth. A tower that has spent a billion years
growing lateral connections into a dense local mesh has high bandwidth.

The deposition zone's computational density is physically motivated: it
is where the bandwidth is. Front-row towers (oldest, most
interconnected, deepest local root mesh) are the network's
highest-bandwidth nodes. Recently arrived towers are still on their
skinny migration trails, slowly building out lateral connections.

This maturation process means the network's computational heartland is
not simply where the most towers are, but where towers have had the
longest time to interconnect. Density of towers and density of
connections are separate variables. The deposition zone maximizes both.

**NEW ENTRIES: VOLCANIC DAMAGE AND RECOVERY**

**4.5.1 Volcanic Damage Spectrum**

*\[NEW --- V2.2\]*

Volcanic events affect towers and the deep-rock network across a
spectrum of severity. The primary threat vector is from below --- magma
intrusion into the fracture networks the deep-rock colony inhabits.

**Minor Incursion**

Magma intrudes into the periphery of a colony's fracture network. The
colony's organisms die in the affected zone; the rest of the network
routes around the damage. Towers above the affected zone experience a
bandwidth dip lasting centuries to millennia as the deep network heals.
Towers remain structurally intact. Surface connections provide continued
(reduced) network participation. Recovery is geological but
straightforward --- the colony recolonizes cooling fractures from its
surviving margins.

**Major Surge (Surface Intact)**

A large magma body kills a significant section of the deep-rock colony
but does not breach the surface. Towers above lose their deep connection
entirely. Surface trails and lateral roots provide continued network
participation at drastically reduced bandwidth. If the network has
absorbed other colonies and alternative deep paths route through
different fracture systems, the damage is a bottleneck, not a severance.
Traffic reroutes. The network is degraded but intact.

**✓ RESOLVED:** Towers can reseed the deep-rock network from above.
Tower roots deposit conductive mineral downward into cooling rock,
recolonizing the fracture network from the surface --- the origin story
running in reverse. However, recovery is extremely slow: the original
colonization was pulled (geothermal gradient actively mobilizing
hydrogen and silicon along fracture paths); reseeding from above is
pushed (tower diverting atmospheric-derived resources downward against
no gradient). Recovery timescale: millions to tens of millions of years.

During recovery, the towers above are running lean. Resources diverted
to underground reconstruction reduce accretion rates. Sacrificial crusts
thin. At the terminator, erosion may begin winning --- the tower shrinks
while regrowing its roots. The scar persists for geological time.

**Caldera Collapse (Surface Breached)**

A super-earth-scale caldera event destroys the deep-rock colony across a
region potentially hundreds of kilometers wide, and breaches the surface
beneath one or more towers. In 1.82g, a kilometer-tall mineralized
lattice with its foundation collapsing does not tip like a tree --- the
open lattice structure shears and crumbles. Sections drop. Millions of
tons of conductive mineral impact the surface, severing surface root
connections across a wide area. Neighboring towers lose lateral
connections. Cascade damage spreads beyond the eruption footprint.

Seismic shock propagates through denser rock in higher gravity. The
lattice is rigid mineral built for compressive self-weight and slow
erosive reshaping, not sudden lateral acceleration. Junctions crack.
Piezoelectric connections shatter. Towers hundreds of kilometers from
the eruption site sustain structural and network damage.

Volcanic ejecta in a 21.8 km scale-height atmosphere reaches extreme
altitudes and persists. The particulate load spikes. At the terminator,
erosion rates surge. Sacrificial crusts across a wide region are pushed
out of equilibrium.

Surface reseeding into rock flooded with cooling magma is not possible
for millions of years. The affected zone is network-dead until the
geology heals.

**4.5.2 Thin Zones**

*\[NEW --- V2.2\]*

**✓ RESOLVED:** Volcanic scars persist as "thin zones" for geological
time --- regions where the deep network is young, shallow, and sparse
relative to surrounding areas. Towers in thin zones are alive,
connected, but bandwidth-starved. They have reduced network support,
shallower root architecture, and thinner margins than equivalent towers
in undamaged regions.

Amorph populations in thin zones are adapted to low connectivity.
Cognitive protocols optimized for self-reliance rather than network
participation. Culturally distinct --- island populations diverging from
mainlands, not because of surface geography but because of a subsurface
wound that predates their species.

Monks visiting thin zones may perceive that the deep network is young
--- regrown, shallow, structurally different from the ancient dense mesh
under the deposition zone heartland. The ground feels "new" in a context
where everything underground is supposed to be older than anything above
it.

During the atmospheric crisis, thin zones are the leading edge of the
extinction wave. Towers there are already running lean: less bandwidth,
less network support, less resilience. When silane depletes, the towers
with the shallowest roots and the thinnest margins die first. Ancient
volcanic scars become the first visible casualties of the atmospheric
crisis. The past kills before anyone understands why.

**⚠ NOTE:** A deep-past super-eruption (hundreds of millions of years

**4.5.3 Network Response to Volcanic Damage**

*\[NEW --- V2.2\]*

The network's automatic response to a bandwidth drop in a region:
neighboring towers with healthy deep connections increase lateral root
growth toward the damaged zone. Surface mineral deposition accelerates
along paths connecting to struggling towers. The network routes around
damage --- wound-healing behavior, not conscious intervention. More
current flowing through remaining connections stimulates more mineral
deposition along those connections, thickening them, increasing
bandwidth. Self-reinforcing repair.

Limits: healthy towers diverting resources to rescue operations weaken
themselves. If the damaged zone is large enough, the rescue effort
drains the surrounding region. The wound gets a bandage but the
neighboring patient gets weaker.
